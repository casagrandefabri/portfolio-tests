SimONE — Clinical Learning Platform — Live session

Most educational experiences ask people to consume information.

Slides.
Videos.
Modules.
Quizzes.

The assumption is simple: if information is delivered, learning will happen.

It rarely works that way.

Observation

Healthcare professionals don't need more content.

They need opportunities to think. To discuss. To challenge assumptions. To make decisions.

The problem with most learning systems isn't the content itself — it's the format. Content consumption places the learner in a passive position. It asks them to receive, not to engage. And in a field where professional judgement matters more than information recall, passive learning misses the point entirely.

Tension
What if learning looked more like practice?

Instead of consuming information, users would navigate clinical situations. Instead of receiving answers, they would make decisions. Instead of completing modules, they would participate.

The shift is subtle but significant. It's the difference between a lecture and a simulation. Between reading about a patient case and arguing about one with a colleague.

System designed

That idea became SimONE.

A learning system designed around interaction, reasoning, and shared discussion. Built for healthcare professionals. Designed for thinking together.

The platform presents clinical cases not as content to be read, but as situations to be navigated. Users encounter a case, make decisions at defined points, and then discuss those decisions in a structured group setting. The system tracks choices, surfaces reasoning patterns, and creates a shared space for professional reflection.

Every design decision prioritised clarity of information, speed of interaction, and the confidence users needed to commit to a position — knowing that the goal wasn't to be correct, but to think clearly.

What changed

The interesting part wasn't the platform.

It was the behaviour.

Once people were invited into the process — asked to reason, to decide, to defend a position — engagement stopped being the objective. It became a consequence.

Participants who had previously moved quickly through content began slowing down. Not because the content was harder, but because it mattered in a different way. The system didn't ask them to remember. It asked them to think.

Engagement stopped being the objective. It became a consequence.

That distinction — between designing for engagement and designing for thinking — shaped every subsequent decision in the project. And it continues to inform how I approach learning systems.
