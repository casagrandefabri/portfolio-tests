People only notice transportation systems when they stop working.

Bus terminals are surprisingly complex environments.

Vehicles arrive late.
Platforms change.
Schedules shift.
Thousands of people move through the same physical space every day.

Behind all of it: a control layer that most passengers never see. A set of decisions made under pressure, with incomplete information, in real time. Gate assignments. Door states. Emergency overrides. Passenger flow management.

When that control layer works, the system is invisible. When it fails, everyone knows.

Observation

Most of the complexity isn't infrastructure.

It's coordination.

Operators managing BRT networks in Colombia face a specific kind of challenge: the information they need to make good decisions is fragmented across systems, displayed in formats that slow down interpretation, and updated at intervals that lag behind reality. A gate can fail. A platform can overload. A schedule can cascade into delays. By the time the operator sees it, the window for a clean intervention has often passed.

Tension
The challenge wasn't designing another dashboard. It was helping people make decisions under pressure.

Dashboards optimise for display. The information is there, but the cost of interpretation is borne by the operator. Every second spent reading is a second not spent deciding.

The question wasn't: how do we show more information? It was: what does an operator need to see right now, given what is currently happening, to act well?

Network overview — station status across the BRT corridor

System designed

Nexus Smart was designed to support gate management and operational coordination across transportation infrastructure in Colombia.

Every workflow in the system existed to answer one question:

What does this operator need to see right now to keep things moving?

That constraint shaped everything. The information hierarchy. The alert system. The state model for each gate (open, closed, manual, error, anti-panic, no network, emergency). The layout of the primary interface — what was always visible, what required a deliberate action to access.

Safety-critical software is, by nature, conservative in its interaction design. Users can't afford to be surprised. The system had to behave predictably under normal conditions, and clearly under abnormal ones. Every state had to be immediately legible. Every action had to be reversible where possible, and clearly warned where it wasn't.

What changed

The project wasn't about interfaces.

It was about flow.

People.
Vehicles.
Timing.
Decisions.

What became clear during the design process was that the operators were not the only users of this system. Every person who boarded a bus, every driver managing a route, every station staff member coordinating access — all of them were affected by the decisions made inside the control interface.

The interface was a point of translation between a complex, moving system and the people responsible for keeping it coherent. Designing it well meant understanding that pressure — and making the interface lighter than the situation it was managing.

Good operational systems don't draw attention to themselves. They create the conditions for everything else to work.

That principle — operational invisibility — became the north star for every design decision in the project. If the system needed to explain itself, it had failed. If an operator could act without thinking about the interface, it had succeeded.
